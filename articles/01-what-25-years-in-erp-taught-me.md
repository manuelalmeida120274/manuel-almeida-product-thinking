# What 25 Years in ERP Taught Me About Building Useful Software

[Back to index](/manuel-almeida-product-thinking/#articles)

---

Over 25 years, I have worked on ERP software from different angles: developer, functional analyst, research and innovation, and later product management. That path changed the way I look at software.

ERP is not glamorous software. It is not usually where people look for viral features or trendy user experiences. But it is where important business work happens: sales, purchases, stock, invoicing, accounting, payroll, reporting, compliance and decision-making.

That makes ERP difficult, but also fascinating. A good ERP needs structure, but it cannot become rigid. It needs flexibility, but it cannot become chaotic. It needs rules, but it cannot make users feel trapped. It needs to serve different companies, sectors, roles and levels of maturity.

These are seven lessons I keep coming back to.

---

## 1. The real problem is often behind the request

Users often ask for something concrete: a report, a field, a button, a configuration option or a new workflow. The request matters, but it is rarely the full problem.

Behind the request there is usually a business need: lack of visibility, too much manual work, a control gap, a compliance issue, an exception in the process or a workaround that became painful over time.

Good product work starts by respecting the request, but not stopping there. The question is not only "what did they ask for?" The real question is "what are they trying to solve?"

This sounds simple. In practice, it is hard. There is always pressure to move fast, accept the request and deliver something visible. But the best product decisions usually start by slowing down enough to understand the real problem.

---

## 2. Simplicity is not the same as removing complexity

Everyone wants software to be simple. That is fair. But in business software, simplicity cannot mean pretending that complexity does not exist.

Companies have rules, exceptions, taxes, permissions, approvals, documents, integrations and different ways of working. A product that ignores this may look simple in a demo and fail in real usage.

Real simplicity is not about removing what is difficult. It is about putting complexity in the right place. The system should handle what it can, guide the user when needed and expose complexity only when it matters.

That is much harder than designing a clean screen.

---

## 3. Flexibility needs boundaries

ERP systems have always needed flexibility because different companies work in different ways. But unlimited flexibility is dangerous.

Too many parameters, too many options and too many exceptions can create products that are difficult to understand, difficult to maintain and difficult to evolve. Flexibility without boundaries often means passing the responsibility for product design to the customer or consultant.

One of the most important product decisions in ERP is deciding what should be standard, what should be configurable and what should not be allowed at all. Good boundaries protect the product, and they also protect the user.

---

## 4. Trust is part of the product

Business software handles things that matter: money, taxes, employee data, stock values, approvals and legal obligations. When something goes wrong, the consequences are real.

That is why trust is not a secondary concern. It is part of the product.

Users need to trust calculations, balances, permissions, integrations and reports. They also need to understand what happened, who did what, when it happened and why the system behaved in a certain way.

Trust is built slowly, through predictable behaviour, clear feedback, consistent rules and good exception handling. It is destroyed quickly by anything that feels opaque, arbitrary or impossible to correct.

With AI, this becomes even more important. A smart answer is not enough. Users need context, explanation and confidence.

---

## 5. Technology only matters when it improves the work

I like technology. I like architecture, platforms, integrations, automation and AI. But technology by itself is not the point.

The point is the work it improves.

A new technology is valuable when it reduces friction, improves decisions, increases confidence, saves time, avoids errors or creates new possibilities for the business.

The user does not care that the architecture is elegant if the closing process is still painful. They do not care that the technology is advanced if reconciliation still requires too much manual checking.

The right question is not "where can we use this technology?" The better question is "where can this genuinely improve the way people work?"

---

## 6. Useful software respects the user's reality

Business users do not work in ideal conditions. They are interrupted. They switch between tasks, systems and conversations. They work under pressure, often with incomplete information, deadlines and operational consequences.

They also have workarounds. Some workarounds are bad habits. Others are intelligent adaptations to problems the product did not solve well enough.

Useful software respects that reality. It does not assume users will always follow the happy path. It makes recovery possible, not just completion. It helps users continue when information is partial.

Workarounds should not be ignored. They are often signals that the product needs to understand better.

---

## 7. Product judgment matters more than feature accumulation

Long-running business products carry the weight of decisions made over many years. Every extra option, every special case and every feature added without enough thought becomes part of the product's future complexity.

More features do not automatically create a better product. Sometimes the right decision is to add. Sometimes it is to simplify. Sometimes it is to say no. Sometimes it is to solve the same problem in a completely different way.

Good product judgment connects business reality, technical constraints, customer needs and long-term product coherence. That judgment becomes even more important with AI, because the temptation to add impressive capabilities is high.

But impressive is not the same as useful.

---

## Wrap-up

After 25 years in ERP, I do not see business software as just screens, features and modules. I see it as a way of shaping how companies work.

Good ERP software connects rules and flexibility, structure and usability, control and productivity, technology and business reality.

AI will change a lot of the experience, but it will not remove the need for good product thinking. It will make product judgment even more important.

In the end, useful software is not the software that looks the most advanced. It is the software that helps people work with more clarity, more confidence and better results.
