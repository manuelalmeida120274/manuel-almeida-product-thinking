# From Menu-Driven ERP to AI-Native Business Platforms

[Back to index](/manuel-almeida-product-thinking/#articles)

---

ERP software has a long history of adapting to new paradigms without fully letting go of old ones.

The move from character-based terminals to graphical interfaces brought menus and windows, but often kept the same navigational logic. The move to web browsers brought new presentation layers on top of familiar business structures. Mobile added another access channel, but did not fundamentally change how most ERP work was done.

What is happening now feels different, not because the technology is more impressive, but because it can change the relationship between the user and the system.

The question is not whether AI will be present in business software. That is already happening. The real question is what kind of product transformation this represents.

To me, the future of ERP is not simply a chatbot added to existing screens. It is a broader evolution from menu-driven software to AI-native business platforms, where users interact through business intentions, contextual insight, intelligent workflows, agents and automated actions with configurable levels of control.

These are five shifts I think define that transition.

---

## 1. From navigation to intention

Traditional ERP is organised around navigation. Users learn where things are: which menu, which screen, which tab, which field sequence. That knowledge becomes expertise, and it takes time to acquire.

It also means the user must understand the software's organisational logic before they can work efficiently.

AI-native platforms can change that starting point. Instead of navigating to a function, the user expresses an intention: prepare the supplier payment run, flag invoices outside tolerance, help close this period, explain why this margin changed.

The system then interprets the intention, gathers the relevant context and handles part of the navigation internally.

This does not mean screens disappear. It means users should not always need to start by knowing where the answer or action is hidden.

The user's cognitive load shifts from knowing where things are to knowing what they want to achieve. That is closer to how people actually think about their work.

---

## 2. From isolated screens to business context

Traditional ERP screens are often atomic. They display a record, allow an action and return a result. Each screen knows its own data, but often very little about why the user opened it, what they were doing before or what happens next in the process.

AI-native systems can maintain and use context across interactions. A system that knows the user is in the middle of a month-end close, that a supplier has repeated discrepancies, that a payment approval has been pending longer than usual, or that stock levels are affecting delivery commitments, can surface more relevant information without forcing the user to manually connect everything.

This is where the product becomes more than a collection of screens. It starts behaving more like a business platform that understands processes, exceptions and relationships between data.

That is technically complex to build well. Maintaining context without overwhelming users with irrelevant suggestions requires careful product design. But the potential is significant: less cross-referencing, less context-switching and more help at the moment the user actually needs it.

---

## 3. From assistance to intelligent automation

The first wave of AI in business software is often assistive: suggestions, summaries, recommendations, anomaly alerts, explanations and draft outputs. These capabilities can add value, but they do not necessarily change the structure of the work. The user still makes every decision and executes every action.

The next shift is toward intelligent automation with configurable levels of control.

Some actions may remain fully manual. Some may be suggested by the system and approved by the user. Others may be executed automatically, monitored by agents and reported back with clear outcomes. Some workflows may run continuously in the background, requiring attention only when there are exceptions, uncertainty or relevant business impact.

This is not about removing human judgment everywhere. It is about deciding where human judgment is necessary, where it is optional and where structured work can be safely delegated.

That is a real product shift. The software stops being only a tool that enables work and starts becoming a system that participates in work.

---

## 4. From menu-driven software to agent-oriented experiences

The menu-driven model has a clear assumption built into it: one user, one screen, one action at a time. The user opens a screen, performs a task, closes it and moves on.

Even well-designed traditional ERP largely operates within this paradigm.

Agent-oriented systems can handle multi-step processes on behalf of users. A user can describe a goal rather than a sequence of actions, and the system can plan, prepare or execute the required steps within defined boundaries.

In some cases, the system may pause for confirmation. In others, it may proceed and report the result. In more sensitive contexts, it may simply prepare the work and wait for the user to decide.

This changes what expertise looks like. In a menu-driven system, expertise is knowing the system. In an agent-oriented system, expertise is also knowing what to delegate, what to review and what to keep under direct control.

Products need to be designed for that new kind of expertise.

---

## 5. From feature design to product judgment

In the menu-driven era, ERP product development was often additive. New regulation required a new field. A new business process required a new screen. Competitive pressure required new features.

The product grew by accumulation, and the main design challenge was managing complexity without making it unworkable.

AI-native products require a different kind of judgment. Adding a capability that works inconsistently is worse than not adding it at all, because it erodes trust. Adding automation that users cannot understand or correct creates accountability problems. Adding intelligence that performs well in demos but fails in edge cases creates frustration in production.

The product discipline required for AI-native software is less about feature coverage and more about deciding what the system should and should not do.

Where is confidence high enough to act? Where should the system suggest but not execute? Where should it ask for clarification? Where should it refuse? Where should the user remain fully in control?

These are product judgment calls, not just engineering decisions. Getting them right requires staying close to the real work users do and the real consequences of getting things wrong.

---

## Wrap-up

The move from menu-driven ERP to AI-native business platforms is not primarily a technology story. The technology is real and significant, but what matters is what it enables and what it requires.

It enables a different relationship between users and their tools, one where the system understands context, handles structured work, surfaces the right information at the right moment and supports action through intelligent workflows and agents.

It also requires product teams to think more carefully about trust, autonomy, control and judgment than was necessary when software was mostly a set of screens and menus.

The ERP will not disappear. The structure remains: data, rules, permissions, workflows, documents, transactions and accountability. What changes is how users experience that structure.

The goal is the same as ever: help users do better work and help companies move their businesses forward, in a fast but sustainable way.
